#### Write here your theoretical framework

The theoretical network of planetary, dwarf planet, and exoplanet classification by the International Astronomical Union (IAU) is based on a systematic framework of criteria that distinguish different types of celestial bodies. A planet, under the IAU's definition, must orbit the Sun, have sufficient mass to assume a nearly round shape, and clear its orbit of other debris. Dwarf planets, such as Pluto, meet the first two conditions but share their orbital zone with other objects. This classification creates a hierarchical network that also includes exoplanets—planets that orbit stars outside our solar system. While exoplanets are not bound by the same strict orbital clearing rule applied to solar system planets, they are similarly categorized based on their size, mass, and orbital characteristics. This network allows astronomers to better understand the diverse range of planetary bodies in the universe, fostering comparative studies across systems while maintaining clear distinctions between different classes of objects.

The methodological approach of the *Exploratorium* is grounded in the examination and construction of hierarchical networks known as lattices. This class of networks is distinguished by its properties as partially ordered sets, establishing a comprehensive hierarchy.

#### Hierarchical networks

Lattices have the property that for any pair of nodes there is only one minimal node and one supreme node. 

#### Formal Concept Analysis

Starting from formal contexts represented by incidence tables (G,M,i), we use the methodology of **Formal Concept Analysis (FCA)**, a mathematical theory based on Birkhoff lattice theory and the Galois connection, to visualize and explore concept lattices. FCA systematically recovers formal concepts (certain ordered pairs of objects and attributes), organizes them hierarchically and represents them as frameworks, offering a deep insight into the structural interdependencies of the concepts.

### IAU's classification of planets

In 2006, the International Astronomical Union (IAU) redefined the criteria for classifying planets, which led to Pluto's reclassification as a "dwarf planet." According to the new criteria, a celestial body must meet three conditions to be considered a planet: it must orbit the Sun, be spherical in shape due to its own gravity, and have cleared its orbit of other debris. Pluto meets the first two criteria but fails the third, as its orbit overlaps with other objects in the Kuiper Belt. This decision sparked significant debate, but it established a clearer distinction between planets and smaller celestial bodies, like dwarf planets.
